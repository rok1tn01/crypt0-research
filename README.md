# crypt0-research


Part 1: The Absolute Fundamentals
These are the core concepts you need to understand the "why" and "what" of crypto.

Cryptocurrency: A digital or virtual form of currency that is secured by cryptography, making it nearly impossible to counterfeit or double-spend. It is typically decentralized and operates on a blockchain.

Blockchain: A decentralized, distributed digital ledger that records transactions across many computers. The record cannot be altered retroactively without the alteration of all subsequent blocks and the consensus of the network. Think of it as a shared, immutable Google Sheet for recording transactions.

Decentralization: The transfer of control and decision-making from a centralized entity (like a government or bank) to a distributed network. This is the core philosophy behind most cryptocurrencies.

Bitcoin (BTC): The first and most valuable cryptocurrency, created in 2009 by the pseudonymous person (or group) Satoshi Nakamoto. It's often called "digital gold."

Ethereum (ETH): A decentralized, open-source blockchain with smart contract functionality. It's the foundation for most of the ecosystem (DeFi, NFTs, etc.) and is often called "the world computer."

Altcoin: Any cryptocurrency other than Bitcoin (e.g., Ethereum, Cardano, Solana).

Token: A digital asset that is built on top of another blockchain. For example, many tokens (like UNI or AAVE) are built on the Ethereum blockchain.

Coin vs. Token: A coin (like BTC or ETH) operates on its own native blockchain. A token is built on an existing blockchain for a specific purpose (governance, utility, etc.).

Wallet: A software program or physical device that stores the public and private keys you need to interact with a blockchain and manage your crypto assets. You don't store "coins" in a wallet; you store the keys to access them on the blockchain.

Public Key: Your wallet's address, similar to an account number. You can share this with others to receive funds.

Private Key: A secret string of letters and numbers that acts as the password to your wallet. Anyone with your private key has full control over your funds. NEVER share it.

Seed Phrase / Recovery Phrase: A list of 12, 18, or 24 words generated by your wallet that can be used to restore access to your funds if you lose your device. This is as important as your private key. Store it securely and offline.

Part 2: How It Works (The Tech Stuff)
This covers the mechanics of how transactions are verified and networks are secured.

Cryptography: The practice of securing information through complex mathematical algorithms, which is the "crypto" in cryptocurrency.

Hash/Hashing: A cryptographic function that takes an input (or 'message') and returns a fixed-size string of bytes. It's a one-way process, like a digital fingerprint for data.

Consensus Mechanism: A protocol that allows all the computers (nodes) in a decentralized network to agree on the state of the blockchain. The two most common are:

Proof-of-Work (PoW): Used by Bitcoin. "Miners" use powerful computers to solve complex math problems to validate transactions and create new blocks. It's very secure but energy-intensive.

Proof-of-Stake (PoS): Used by Ethereum 2.0, Cardano, etc. "Validators" lock up (stake) their own crypto to validate transactions and create new blocks. It's faster and more energy-efficient.

Mining: The process in a PoW system where miners compete to solve a cryptographic puzzle. The winner adds a new block to the blockchain and is rewarded with new coins (the "block reward") and transaction fees.

Staking: The process of locking up crypto holdings in a PoS network to help secure it and earn rewards.

Node: Any computer that connects to a blockchain network and helps maintain it by validating and relaying transactions.

Smart Contract: Self-executing contracts with the terms of the agreement directly written into code. They automatically execute when predetermined conditions are met. They are the building blocks for DeFi and DApps.

Gas Fee: A transaction fee on the Ethereum network (and others) required to execute a transaction or smart contract. It's paid to the network validators.

Part 3: The Ecosystem & Use Cases
This is where the technology gets applied, creating a whole new financial and digital world.

DeFi (Decentralized Finance): An ecosystem of financial applications (lending, borrowing, trading) built on blockchains that aim to be open, permissionless, and without traditional intermediaries like banks.

NFT (Non-Fungible Token): A unique, non-interchangeable unit of data stored on a blockchain that certifies ownership of a digital or physical asset (like art, music, or collectibles).

DApp (Decentralized Application): An application that runs on a decentralized network (like Ethereum) instead of a central server. They are typically open-source and not controlled by a single entity.

Stablecoin: A type of cryptocurrency whose value is pegged to a stable asset, like the US Dollar (e.g., USDT, USDC). They are designed to reduce volatility.

DAO (Decentralized Autonomous Organization): An organization represented by rules encoded as a computer program that is transparent, controlled by the organization's members, and not influenced by a central government. It's like a co-op run by code and member votes.

CeFi (Centralized Finance): Traditional financial services in the crypto space, like centralized exchanges (Coinbase, Binance) where a company acts as an intermediary.

CEX (Centralized Exchange): A platform operated by a company to buy and sell cryptocurrency (e.g., Coinbase, Binance, Kraken). They are easy to use but require you to trust the company with your funds.

DEX (Decentralized Exchange): A peer-to-peer exchange that allows direct crypto trades without an intermediary (e.g., Uniswap, PancakeSwap). Users trade directly from their own wallets.

Web3: The vision for a new, decentralized version of the internet built on blockchain technology, where users own their data and identity.

Part 4: Trading & Investment Terms
The language of the markets.

HODL: A famous misspelling of "hold" that has become a mantra in the crypto community, meaning to hold onto your crypto for the long term despite price volatility.

FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out): The anxiety that leads people to buy an asset when its price is rising rapidly, often leading to buying at the peak.

FUD (Fear, Uncertainty, and Doubt): Negative, and often misleading, information spread to create panic and cause prices to drop.

Whale: An individual or entity that holds a very large amount of a particular cryptocurrency, capable of moving the market with their trades.

Market Cap (Capitalization): The total value of a cryptocurrency. It's calculated as (Current Price) x (Total Circulating Supply). It's a key metric for comparing the relative size of different cryptos.

Circulating Supply: The number of coins or tokens that are publicly available and circulating in the market.

Bull Market / Bullish: A period of rising prices and optimistic sentiment.

Bear Market / Bearish: A period of falling prices and pessimistic sentiment.

DYOR (Do Your Own Research): A common disclaimer reminding investors not to blindly follow others' advice but to investigate projects themselves.

Rug Pull: A scam where developers abandon a project and run away with investors' funds. Common in DeFi with anonymous teams.

Part 5: Advanced Concepts
Once you're comfortable with the basics, you'll encounter these.

Layer 1 (L1): The base blockchain network (e.g., Bitcoin, Ethereum, Solana). They handle the core consensus and security.

Layer 2 (L2): A scaling solution built on top of a Layer 1 blockchain to make it faster and cheaper (e.g., Lightning Network for Bitcoin, Polygon for Ethereum).

Oracle: A service that brings real-world data (like price feeds, weather data) onto the blockchain for smart contracts to use.

Airdrop: The distribution of free tokens to a community, often as a reward for early adoption or participation.

Yield Farming / Liquidity Mining: The practice of locking up crypto in a DeFi protocol to earn rewards, often in the form of additional tokens.

Liquidity Pool: A crowdsourced pool of tokens locked in a smart contract that facilitates trading on DEXs by providing liquidity.

APY (Annual Percentage Yield): The real rate of return earned on an investment, taking into account the effect of compounding interest. Commonly used in staking and DeFi.

TVL (Total Value Locked): A metric that measures the total amount of assets deposited in a DeFi protocol. It's a key indicator of the protocol's popularity and health.

Sharding: A database partitioning technique used by some L1 blockchains to improve scalability and transaction speed.

Zero-Knowledge Proof (ZK-Proof): A cryptographic method where one party can prove to another that a statement is true, without revealing any information beyond the validity of the statement itself. A key technology for privacy and scaling.

Memecoin: A cryptocurrency that is based on an internet meme or created as a joke (e.g., Dogecoin, Shiba Inu). They are highly speculative and volatile.
